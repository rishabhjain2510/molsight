Understanding the quantitative measures that define pharmacological potential. A detailed reference for the descriptors and drug-likeness rules used in MolSight.

Lipinski's Rule of Five

A rule of thumb to evaluate drug-likeness. Poor absorption is more likely when there are more than 5 H-bond donors, 10 H-bond acceptors, MW over 500 Da, and LogP over 5.

Status Standardized
Author Lipinski, 1997
Criticality HIGH

Molecular Weight

MW

The sum of the atomic masses of all atoms in a molecule, expressed in daltons (Da) or g/mol.

Why it matters

Larger molecules are harder to absorb across biological membranes. Oral drugs typically stay below 500 Da to pass through the gastrointestinal tract intact.

Threshold

≤ 500 Da

Lipophilicity (LogP)

Partition

The base-10 logarithm of a compound's partition coefficient between n-octanol and water — a measure of how much a molecule prefers a fatty environment over a watery one.

Why it matters

Governs how easily a drug crosses lipid bilayer membranes and how it is handled by the liver. The sweet spot for most oral drugs is roughly 1–3.

Threshold

≤ 5

H-Bond Donors

HBD

The count of NH and OH groups — atoms that can donate a hydrogen bond. Each donor increases affinity for water and reduces lipid partitioning.

Lipinski Limit ≤ 5

H-Bond Acceptors

HBA

The count of nitrogen and oxygen atoms — atoms that can accept a hydrogen bond. A high acceptor count hampers passive membrane permeation.

Lipinski Limit ≤ 10

Topological Polar Surface Area

TPSA

The surface area (Å²) contributed by polar atoms — primarily nitrogen and oxygen with attached hydrogens — calculated from 2D topology without a 3D conformation.

Why it matters

One of the best predictors of passive intestinal absorption and blood–brain barrier penetration. CNS drugs require a tighter ≤ 90 Å² cutoff.

Oral / CNS

≤ 140 / ≤ 90 Å²

Rotatable Bonds

Flexibility

The number of non-terminal, non-ring single bonds around which free rotation is possible. Ring bonds and bonds to terminal groups (CH₃, NH₂) are excluded.

Why it matters

Highly flexible molecules pay a large entropic cost when adopting a binding conformation. Veber et al. (2002) showed ≤ 10 rotatable bonds correlates with better oral bioavailability.

Veber Rule

≤ 10

Lipinski Rule of Five

Drug-likeness filter

An empirical guideline proposed by Christopher Lipinski (Pfizer, 1997) that predicts whether a compound is likely to have adequate oral bioavailability based on four simple physicochemical filters.

The Four Rules

RuleCriterionRationale
1MW ≤ 500 DaLarge molecules cannot passively cross intestinal membranes
2LogP ≤ 5Highly lipophilic compounds have poor aqueous solubility
3HBD ≤ 5Excess donors impede membrane permeation
4HBA ≤ 10Excess acceptors increase hydrophilicity and efflux

A compound is considered drug-like if it violates at most one rule. Violating two or more significantly reduces the probability of good oral absorption. The Ro5 does not predict potency, toxicity, or metabolic stability.

Notable exceptions exist: natural products, antibiotics, and compounds that rely on active transport often violate Ro5 yet remain successful drugs.

Reference: Lipinski CA et al., Adv. Drug Deliv. Rev. 1997, 23, 3–25.

All thresholds are guidelines derived from statistical analysis of known drug molecules, not absolute rules. Context — target class, route of administration, and intended patient population — always matters in real drug design.
